On Layoffs

Posted on Mar 13, 2024 DRAFT
tl;dr: Life is rlly hard, k?

Believe it or not, I wasn’t always a tech guy.

In my early 20’s, I was obsessed with music, to the point of obtaining a Bachelor’s of Music with a concentration in Sound Recording. I wanted desperatly to be a cool studio musician/engineer like Steve Albini or Nigel Goodrich. This period was roughly in the years 2008-2013; in my youthful naiveté, I was unconcerned with the recession that had rocked my country. It also helped that I had no money or career to lose, so I simply followed my passion.

Alas, like many who chased the music dragon, it did not work out for me. My own dreams began to change in my 20’s as well. The “starving artist” lifestyle that had once seemed so vital and real had begun to feel more like poverty and total lack of mobility. The “side gig” of waiting tables to make ends meet had turned into my only reliable source of income, and at a certain point, I had to make a difficult decision: what next?

Fish In A Barrel

Give a man some code and he’ll… um… code… for a day.

Teach a man to code and he’ll… uh… code for his or family or something.

At this point in the story, it’s the mid-2010s.

The learn to code meme by now has fully saturated the cultural collective unconscious, and so I resign myself to learning the drudgery of making computers do stuff. It seems like that’s where all the other failed creative types end up, so I might as well hop on the bandwagon.

This turns out to be lengthy process for me, involving Community College, learning calculus in my mid-20s, grad school, and Mainframe computers. Eventually though, it pays off, and in January 2020, I end up landing a gig at a Large Corporation™ working on an e-commerce enterprise search platform.

For the first time in my life, I’m challenged by a job.

This is quite the upheaval. Other things have been challenging for me, but they have never correlated to, uh, the thing that makes me money. I find myself relishing the challenge, and dig into my new career with gusto. It helps that the pandemic has begun, and I find myself with free time to spare to hone my new craft.

A year, then two go by.

Imposter syndrome, something I’m very familiar with from my time as a musician, plagues me with a dogged determination throughout this phase (and still does, if I’m honest). I don’t let it get me down though, as I finally have the thing that I’ve been grinding for years for: real, actual experience as a Software Engineer.

I’ve found my golden ticket. No more worries about retirement, providing for ageing parents, not amounting to anything, etc. I even have begun to enjoy this new career. It’s interesting, and empowering in a way that I never experienced in other pursuits. Hell, it’s even fun - Vim, for example, is practically text editing as a game.

Reality Bytes

My first real foray into programming began in 2015 with some Java classes at Community College, so my knowledge of programming was of the hard-earned and badly-optimized variety. I also didn’t have the advantage of going to a school with a rich culture steeped in programming lore and practice; my exposure to “hacker culture” came mostly from reading Neal Stephenson books and listening to the Shop Talk podcast. Heck, I hadn’t even heard of hackernews till after finding my first programming job.

So, the aforementioned “grind” was very real for me during those years, and I really didn’t even like the craft of programming yet. It was more of a means to an end.

Here’s the thing.

The whole reason I went into tech can be boiled down into a desire for stability.

Tech was (and is) sold as a meritocracy.

If you can put in the work, and learn to deliver, then you can find success. My music school days taught me that I could put in the hours to learn stuff, and so it seemed like a natural fit. I was mostly right about that.

Where I was wrong was thinking that if I broke past a certain threshold, then I would be set for life.

This month, my manager was laid off and my six person team was downsized to two devs who now report to an offshore team. I suspect this is mostly due to the corporate policy du jour surrounding tech layoffs recently, but I’m not blind to the fact that an offshore dev costs significantly less than I do.

Did you catch that?

…an offshore dev costs significantly less than I do.

At the end of the day, I was just an expense. And that’s ok, honestly.

Wrap

I’ve mentioned several times in this article that I started in music. Music rewards excellence. I would argue that excellence actually is the “product” that music delivers.

Tech actually doesn’t reward excellence per se, or at least most jobs in tech don’t.

It’s seductive to think about your work as having quantifiable “excellence”, but the reality is that it’s is usually just a means to an end for most companies.

It’s like your home router - you don’t actually care about the damn thing, you just want your internet to work. That’s how most businesses are going to view what you do as a Software Engineer. They are going to want what you provide to (1) work and (2) be as cheap as possible.
